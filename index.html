The art of
making choices

Every choice is two things at once: a small beginning, and a quiet funeral for the lives you won't now live. We spend most of our effort on the beginning — weighing, comparing, optimizing — and almost none on the part that actually hurts, which is the letting go.

More options were supposed to make us freer. Mostly they make us slower. A menu of forty dishes is not forty times the pleasure of a menu of four; it's the same dinner with more anxiety attached. To decide is, literally, to cut off — and the relief on the far side of a real decision is the feeling of finally putting the knife down.

So I've stopped trying to choose perfectly. Perfect assumes you could have known, and you almost never could. What I aim for now is to choose in a way I can stand behind later, even if it turns out wrong — to close the door gently, on purpose, and walk. A life only takes a shape once some doors are allowed to stay shut.
